# The 12 Principles — Diagnostic Reference

Each principle: definition · **missing when…** · **add it by…**

## 1. Squash & Stretch
Objects deform to convey weight, mass, and speed while preserving volume.
- Missing when: a ball or logo moves rigidly, feels stiff and made of stone.
- Add it by: stretch along the direction of fast motion, squash on impact/landing; keep total volume constant so it doesn't look like it's growing.

## 2. Anticipation
A small opposite move that prepares the audience for the main action.
- Missing when: motion starts abruptly from a dead stop; feels robotic.
- Add it by: 2–4 frames of counter-movement (a wind-up back before jumping forward, a dip before a rise) before the main move.

## 3. Staging
Direct the eye to what matters; one clear idea at a time.
- Missing when: multiple things move at once and the eye doesn't know where to look.
- Add it by: isolate the key action, quiet everything else, use contrast (motion vs. stillness) to point the eye.

## 4. Straight-Ahead vs. Pose-to-Pose
Two working methods: frame-by-frame continuous (straight-ahead) vs. key poses then fill (pose-to-pose).
- Missing when: fluid effects feel stiff, or planned moves feel loose and off-model.
- Add it by: use pose-to-pose for controlled, on-brand moves (define key poses, ease between them); use straight-ahead for organic effects (fire, smoke, energy).

## 5. Follow-Through & Overlapping Action
Parts keep moving after the object stops; different parts move at different rates.
- Missing when: everything starts and stops on the exact same frame — reads as one rigid block.
- Add it by: let trailing elements (tails, cloth, secondary shapes) settle 2–6 frames after the main body; stagger their stops.

## 6. Slow In & Slow Out
Ease around keyframes — accelerate out of and into poses.
- Missing when: constant velocity between poses (linear); mechanical look.
- Add it by: apply ease-out on departure and ease-in on arrival (see motion-easing for concrete curves).

## 7. Arcs
Natural movement follows curved paths, not straight lines.
- Missing when: elements travel in dead-straight lines between points; feels artificial.
- Add it by: route motion along a gentle arc; rotate slightly through the path so it curves.

## 8. Secondary Action
Supporting motion that reinforces the main action without stealing focus.
- Missing when: the main move is technically fine but flat, lacks richness.
- Add it by: add a subordinate motion (a slight bounce, a shadow shift, a trailing accent) that supports — never competes with — the primary action.

## 9. Timing
The number of frames a move takes defines weight and mood.
- Missing when: moves feel too fast (weightless) or too slow (sluggish).
- Add it by: tune frame counts to the intended weight — heavy = more frames, snappy = fewer (see motion-timing for budgets).

## 10. Exaggeration
Push the action beyond literal reality for clarity and appeal.
- Missing when: motion is accurate but lifeless, too timid to read.
- Add it by: amplify the key pose or accent — bigger anticipation, stronger overshoot — while staying believable.

## 11. Solid Drawing
Respect volume, weight, and dimension; forms hold together in space.
- Missing when: shapes distort inconsistently or lose their form during motion.
- Add it by: keep proportions and perspective consistent through the move; deformations should still read as the same object.

## 12. Appeal
The design is pleasing, clear, and has charisma.
- Missing when: the motion works but is forgettable, generic.
- Add it by: strengthen silhouette and readability, add a signature accent, remove clutter that dilutes the moment.
